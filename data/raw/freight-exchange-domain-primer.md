# Freight Exchange Fundamentals: Actors, Loads, and Matching

## What A Freight Exchange Is

A freight exchange is a two-sided digital marketplace in which one side publishes transport demand and the other offers vehicle capacity, while the platform supplies discovery, matching, and the commercial rails that let strangers transact safely. Demand arrives as loads: shipments described by origin, destination, weight, dimensions, equipment requirement, and a loading date window. Supply arrives as capacity: vehicles with a known body type, a current or planned position, and a date on which they become free. The platform's core product is the reduction of search cost between the two.

The model is usually contrasted with traditional brokerage. A broker acts as a principal, buying transport from a carrier and selling it to a shipper, holding both relationships and earning an opaque margin, so its capacity is bounded by the phone calls its staff can make. An exchange instead keeps counterparties visible to each other and earns from access, transaction fees, or attached financial services, so its throughput is bounded by software rather than headcount; the trade-off is that a broker absorbs coordination and risk an exchange must rebuild as vetting and settlement features. The private fleet is the third option, trading capital intensity and empty-running risk for control. The marketplace model exists because European road freight is structurally fragmented — a long tail of operators running a handful of vehicles each, none covering every corridor — so matching cost dominates and a neutral aggregator is cheaper than either brokerage headcount or fleet ownership.

## Core Actors And Roles

Four roles account for most activity on a freight exchange, and each wants something different. The shipper is the party whose goods must move — a manufacturer, retailer, or distributor — and optimizes for reliable pickup inside a promised window, predictable cost, and evidence of what happened to the cargo. The carrier moves the freight, owning or leasing vehicles and employing or contracting drivers, and optimizes for utilization: keeping trucks loaded and finding a return leg before the outbound leg is delivered. Those objectives are related but not symmetric, which is why marketplace design cannot give both sides identical mechanics.

The freight forwarder arranges transport on a shipper's behalf, bundling legs across modes and jurisdictions, and so appears as both demand and supply, consuming carrier capacity while presenting itself as the responsible counterparty upstream. Logistics service provider, usually abbreviated LSP, is the umbrella term for any participant offering logistics services on the platform, whether haulage, forwarding, warehousing, or a combination. A large European exchange may serve 8,500+ logistics service providers across Europe, roughly the density at which network effects become real, because cross-border coverage only works when enough operators are present in enough markets that a load posted in one country reliably finds capacity in another. Two secondary roles matter for system design: drivers supply position data and proof of delivery through a mobile application, and dispatchers, the heaviest daily users of search, shape read traffic far more than booking volume does.

## The Load Lifecycle

A load moves through a small number of states, and modelling that progression as an explicit state machine rather than a set of implicit flags is what keeps a freight platform auditable. A load is first posted by a shipper or forwarder, visible to searching carriers with its commercial terms still open. Posting implies no commitment, and the state carries an expiry, because a load whose loading window has passed must stop appearing in results without a human remembering to remove it. It becomes matched when a carrier expresses concrete interest, typically by quoting a price or accepting a tender — a supply-side transition, and one best modelled as reversible, since several carriers may sit matched against one load while the shipper decides.

The load becomes booked when the demand side confirms one candidate. That is the first irreversible commercial transition, so invariants must be enforced hardest there: a load can be booked exactly once, and confirmation must be idempotent, because a retried request must never create a second booking. It enters in transit when the vehicle collects the goods, usually triggered by the driver or by a telematics signal rather than an office user, and only then are position updates and an ETA meaningful. It becomes delivered when the consignee receives the goods and proof of delivery is captured — an operational fact, not yet a financial one. The final transition to settled is triggered by the payment process once delivery evidence is accepted and the invoice cleared, commonly lagging delivery by the thirty to sixty day terms customary in road freight.

## Load Matching Mechanics

Matching is the search problem at the centre of a freight exchange, and its inputs are highly structured. Carriers filter primarily by lane, meaning an origin and destination pair expressed as a radius around a point or a set of postal or country regions, because a dispatcher thinks in corridors rather than exact addresses. They then filter by vehicle type and body — curtainsider, box, refrigerated, tipper, tanker — since a mismatch makes a load unbookable regardless of price. Weight and dimensional constraints follow, including loading metres and whether the cargo is stackable, and finally the loading date window, which expires and therefore prunes the candidate set most aggressively. Ranking sits on top of filtering: pure recency rewards spam posting, so useful ranking blends freshness with proximity to the vehicle's declared position, rate against a lane benchmark, and counterparty reputation. Backhaul optimization is the highest-value refinement, since a carrier delivering into a region wants its return leg found before the outbound leg completes.

Scale changes the implementation, not the semantics. At 5,000 requests per second, matching must be index-backed rather than scan-based: the candidate set has to be reachable through composite indexes over geography, equipment, and date, with pagination bounded so a broad query cannot degrade into a traversal. At that volume the read path is read-mostly and heavily repeated, so a denormalized search projection maintained separately from the transactional record is the common design. Those index entries are then invalidated by domain events — a load booked, withdrawn, or expired emits an event that rewrites the affected entries — rather than by periodic rebuilds, because a stale hit that is no longer bookable is the failure mode carriers punish hardest.

## Trust And Vetting

Trust is the hard problem in a two-sided freight marketplace, because the transaction is high value, physically irreversible, and performed by a counterparty the other side has usually never met. A shipper handing over a full trailer is extending real credit and real risk to an operator selected from a list, so verification is not a compliance afterthought but a core product surface, and its rigour is much of what separates an exchange from an open classified-ads board. Vetting typically begins at onboarding, where a participant's legal existence is checked against company registries, its transport operator licence is validated against the relevant national authority, and identity documents are reviewed. Insurance is the second pillar: goods-in-transit cover and carrier liability policies must be evidenced and re-checked on expiry rather than captured once at signup, since a lapsed policy looks identical to a valid one in a store that holds only a document.

Reputation forms the continuous layer over that periodic one. Ratings collected after each completed shipment, weighted by recency and by the transaction volume behind them, capture what vetting cannot: whether the operator shows up on time, communicates during delays, and returns paperwork. Freight reputation systems face the familiar cold-start and retaliation problems, so ratings are commonly restricted to counterparties in a completed booking and supplemented with platform-observed behaviour such as cancellation rate and proof-of-delivery timeliness. The trade-off is real, because strict vetting suppresses supply growth, and every exchange must choose deliberately where on that curve to sit.

## Key Domain Vocabulary

A lane is a recurring origin-destination corridor, and carriers price and plan around lanes rather than individual addresses, because a corridor run weekly has known transit times, known return-leg availability, and known cost. Lane thinking is why search filters accept regions and radii instead of exact points. Spot freight and contract freight are the two commercial modes: spot freight is a one-off shipment priced at whatever the market bears on the day, which is what public postings mostly consist of, while contract freight is a pre-agreed rate over a period that gives the shipper price stability and the carrier guaranteed volume. Most large shippers run a mix of the two.

FTL, full truckload, means one consignment occupies the vehicle for a direct run from collection to delivery; LTL, less-than-truckload, means several consignments from different shippers share the vehicle and pass through consolidation hubs, lowering cost per shipment while increasing transit time and handling risk. That distinction changes the data model, since an LTL shipment has a route with intermediate stops rather than a single leg. A tender is an offer of a load to a specific carrier at agreed terms, usually against a contract; unlike an open posting it is directed and time-limited, and a decline commonly cascades to the next carrier in a ranked routing guide. Proof of delivery, abbreviated POD, is the confirmation that the consignee received the goods, now typically a photograph or signature capture from the driver's application, and the artefact that triggers invoicing and settlement. A backhaul is a return-leg load that stops a vehicle running empty after delivery, and because empty running is pure cost, backhaul availability is often the strongest single determinant of whether a carrier accepts an outbound load.

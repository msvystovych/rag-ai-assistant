## Conclusion — what gave the biggest effect

Metadata filtering delivered the reliability gain and hybrid search delivered the precision gain; the combination is strictly better than either alone. Filter-only lifted top-3 expected-document precision from 0.889 to 0.926 by evicting foreign-document chunks (q09's CQRS leak disappears) while never changing a correct result — the conservative zero-or-tie → unfiltered rule means it cannot misfire on ambiguous queries. Hybrid-only produced the single largest per-query win — q05, where inference stays unfiltered and BM25's lexical match on the ledger/immutable-events vocabulary promoted two genuinely better chunks — but on its own it also REGRESSED q09 (a foreign CQRS chunk reached top-1 on the word 'event') and leaked a migration chunk into q06's top-3, dropping the top-1 hit rate to 0.89. Combined, the filter constrains exactly that leakage while hybrid re-ranks within the filtered set: top-3 precision 0.963 with the top-1 hit rate back at 1.00. So the biggest effect comes from metadata filtering — it both adds precision and removes hybrid's failure mode — while the biggest single-query improvement comes from BM25 on the one query filtering cannot touch. Two honest caveats: with four document types mapping 1:1 to four documents, a correct filter is equivalent to selecting the right document, so its measured effect is an upper bound a larger corpus would not reproduce as strongly; and q08 shows the breadcrumb title-token boost reordering hits within the right document to a slightly worse top-1 — hybrid is a net win in aggregate, not uniformly per query.
